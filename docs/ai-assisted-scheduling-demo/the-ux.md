The demo should feel like:

> **“Oh — this is easier than what I do now.”**

Not:

> “Here’s a powerful system with lots of steps.”

---

# The experience principle

For Molly, the product should feel like **guided relief**.

She should feel:

* the system already understands her world
* she only makes a few simple moves
* the hard thinking happens for her
* changes are absorbed without drama

That matches her real pain: constant rework from shifting constraints and new students. 

---

# What “effortless” means in the demo

## Molly should only do 3 simple things

1. **Review her students**
2. **Click to generate**
3. **Add one new student**

That’s it.

Everything else should feel automatic, explained, and calm.

---

# Revised Molly interaction model

## Scene 1 — Welcome

No action.

AI says:

> “Let’s build your schedule from the students you already support.”

This lowers anxiety immediately.

---

## Scene 2 — Student roster

Very light interaction.

She can glance at student cards, but she does **not** need to edit a bunch of fields on camera.

Best pattern:

* preloaded student list
* constraints already visible
* one obvious primary button

Primary action:

```text
[ Generate Groups ]
```

---

## Scene 3 — Grouping result

No extra work.

She clicks once, then the system:

* groups students
* explains why
* highlights 1:1 students
* shows confidence / reasoning lightly

This should feel like:

> “It already sees what I see.”

---

## Scene 4 — Build schedule

One click:

```text
[ Build Weekly Schedule ]
```

Then the system does the rest.

No drag-and-drop.
No manual slotting.
No fiddly adjustments.

That would introduce accidental complexity instead of removing it. 

---

## Scene 5 — Schedule appears

Passive.

The grid appears already solved:

* required minutes met
* conflicts avoided
* clean visual reassurance

This is the “I didn’t have to fight it” moment.

---

## Scene 6 — Add a new student

This is the only place where input matters.

But even here, keep it minimal.

Instead of a full form, use a tiny guided drawer:

```text
New student
Name: Mason
Grade: 3
Minutes: 120
Support: Group if possible

[ Add to Schedule ]
```

Only 4 fields.
Nothing else.

---

## Scene 7 — Rebalance

This should **not** feel like another task.

Best version:

* Molly clicks **Add to Schedule**
* system automatically rebalances
* affected rows glow
* new group placement animates in
* narration explains what changed

No separate “Rebalance” button unless you want a more explicit theatrical moment.

For realism, automatic is better.

---

## Scene 8 — Reassurance / before vs after

Passive close.

This is where the system tells her:

* no full rebuild
* no lost constraints
* no starting over

The emotional close is not “look how advanced this is.”

It is:

> **“This stays manageable, even when things change.”**

---

# The emotional arc she should feel

## Start

“Yeah, this is my mess.”

## After grouping

“Okay, that’s already useful.”

## After scheduling

“Oh, that saved time.”

## After new student / rebalance

“This is the part I actually need.”

That last one is the sale.

---

# The core UX rule

Every time you’re tempted to add an interaction, ask:

> **Would Molly really want to do this herself, or would she want the system to handle it?**

For the demo, most of the answer should be:

> the system handles it.

---

# Best final interaction count

## Molly actively does:

* **Generate Groups**
* **Build Weekly Schedule**
* **Add to Schedule**

## The system automatically does:

* grouping logic
* schedule generation
* conflict checks
* rebalance
* explanation

That will make the experience feel calm, obvious, and believable.
