The demo should stop feeling like **“here are some UI screens”** and start feeling like **“this is an operating system for a real business.”**

## The shift to make

For Thomas, the winning story is not the screens themselves. It is:

> **one signal enters the system, and the platform turns it into coordinated operational action across customers, technicians, compliance, and reporting at scale.**

That means every scene should answer one question:

**How does this reduce manual coordination when there are 10 sites, 100 sites, or 1,000 sites?**

The choreography you uploaded already supports this structure well, especially the sequence from signal recognition, to context investigation, to onboarding, field capture, triage, compliance interpretation, and decisioning. 

## What Thomas should feel by the end

He should walk away thinking:

* this is not just a dashboard
* this is how a small service business becomes a scalable compliance operation
* the system standardizes judgment, documentation, and follow-through
* one team can manage many more sites without losing control

## The demo story to tell

### 1. Start with the operating problem, not the interface

Open with something like:

> “Imagine you’re not managing a few generators manually anymore. You’re managing a growing book of residential and commercial sites, each with service intervals, alerts, compliance requirements, and reporting obligations. This system is how that becomes operationally manageable.”

That aligns directly with the internal-ops-first framing in the MVP materials, where the platform is positioned as the daily command center for onboarding customers, tracking equipment, logging visits, managing compliance, generating reports, and monitoring exceptions. 

### 2. Scene 1 should establish the system as a control plane

The operations dashboard is not “metrics.” It is the **live queue of business risk and work orchestration**.

Say:

> “This is where the business runs each morning. The system is consolidating schedule, alerts, reports due, and compliance exceptions into one queue. So instead of Thomas remembering what matters, the system surfaces what matters.”

That matches the dashboard wireframe: visits, reports due, compliance issues, monitoring alerts, schedule, alerts/tasks, and KPI strip.  

### 3. Move immediately from signal to context

When you go to the customer detail screen, the point is not “we can view profile data.”

The point is:

> “Every alert is attached to service history, monitoring history, compliance history, documents, and prior actions — so operators don’t need to reconstruct context from memory or separate systems.”

That is exactly how the customer detail screen is structured: one record unifying service history, monitoring status, compliance status, reports, files, and timeline.  

### 4. Show that onboarding creates the structure for future scale

The onboarding screen should be narrated as **data model creation**, not admin entry.

Use language like:

> “This isn’t just adding a customer. This is defining the operational template for that site: plan type, equipment profile, monitoring setup, and compliance flags. Once that structure exists, the rest of the workflow can be automated.”

That matches the onboarding wireframe: customer type, equipment details, plan assignment, and compliance flags such as UST involvement, EPA run-hour logging, monthly threshold verification, annual reporting, and load-bank applicability.  

### 5. Make field work the engine of structured data capture

The service visit report should be framed as the moment where technician work becomes reusable operational intelligence.

Say:

> “A technician visit is no longer a one-off report. It becomes structured data that updates customer history, compliance posture, evidence records, and future reporting automatically.”

That is the real leverage point in the MVP notes: once structured data is captured, it can generate visit reports, monthly summaries, annual reports, and compliance records. 

### 6. Position alert triage as queue management, not alert viewing

In the monitoring/triage scene, emphasize:

> “At scale, the problem is not getting alerts. It’s deciding what requires action, what can be resolved, what becomes compliance work, and what becomes a service task.”

The triage screen already reflects that operationally: filtering alerts, prioritizing by severity, showing selected alert detail, and converting it into tasks like create compliance task, flag customer record, include in monthly report, or schedule load bank evaluation.  

### 7. Make compliance tracker the defensibility moment

This is probably the key screen for Thomas.

Narrate it as:

> “This is where the business stops being just maintenance and becomes a compliance platform. The system tracks obligations continuously, shows what is satisfied, what is missing, and what action is next.”

That maps directly to the compliance tracker’s purpose: EPA run-hour logging, exercise vs outage separation, UST documentation review, NFPA threshold handling, annual compliance reports, filing status, and records retention.  

### 8. Treat the load bank screen as decision standardization

This is where you show that expertise becomes repeatable logic.

Say:

> “Instead of relying on memory or judgment in the moment, the system applies the rule consistently across months of data and recommends the next action. That means Thomas’s expertise becomes scalable across more operators and more sites.”

The load bank decision screen already expresses this clearly through threshold history, rule result, recommended timing, and estimated charge.  

## The exact scalable operating loop to emphasize

Throughout the demo, keep repeating this loop:

**Signal → Context → Triage → Field Action → Compliance Update → Report Output**

That chain is explicitly present in the choreography as the “Signal → Context → Intelligence → Action” motion. 

That is the scalable story.

Because when Thomas sees that loop, he sees:

* fewer dropped balls
* standardized workflows
* faster training of staff
* better documentation
* more commercial credibility
* more sites handled per operator

> “This is where domain expertise becomes a repeatable operational rule.”
